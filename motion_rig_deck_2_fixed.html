Motion Axes

Yaw, pitch, and roll.

Before explaining the mechanism, these are the three ways a simulator can rotate. This design starts with pitch + roll because they give the biggest driving feel for the least cost and complexity.

This airplane GIF shows the three motions more clearly: pitch tips the nose up/down, roll leans wing-to-wing, and yaw rotates left/right.

Pitch

Pitch is the forward/back tilt. In a driving sim, this is the cue that helps braking, acceleration, hills, and weight shifting feel more believable.

Roll

Roll is the side-to-side lean. This is the cue that helps cornering feel real, because your body expects some left/right lean in turns and over curbs.

Yaw

Yaw would rotate the whole simulator left/right. It is useful, but it needs a much bigger moving base and much more structure than this first version.

This Design Uses First
Pitch Roll

Pitch gives the brake / acceleration feeling. Roll gives the cornering / leaning feeling. Those are the two most important driving cues for a compact sim, so they are the best first choice for the money.

Why Yaw Waits Until Later

Yaw would mean rotating the whole simulator on a bigger base. That adds more structure, more torque, more wiring complexity, and more cost before it adds as much useful feeling as pitch + roll.

  • +More expensive than pitch + roll for a first build
  • +Harder mechanically and electrically
  • +Pitch + roll already cover the biggest driving cues

The Project

A cockpit that physically tilts.

This is the real Onshape Assem4 motion. The lower base stays on the floor, the upper frame carries the driver controls, and the rear motors move that upper frame through the blue linkage rods.

1. Fixed Lower Base

The bottom frame is the part that stays on the floor. It holds the motors, power parts, and the rest of the structure.

2. Moving Upper Frame

The top frame is the part that carries the seat, wheel, and pedals. This is the part that tilts to create motion cues.

3. Front U-Joint

The front U-joint keeps the moving frame attached while still letting it tilt. It acts like the main front hinge of the rig.

4. Rear Motors + Blue Linkages

The rear motors spin crank arms, and the blue rods push and pull the back of the moving frame. That is what creates pitch and roll.

What I Learn

Mechanical engineering concepts.

Every part is a lesson. You can't pick random numbers — the geometry has to be calculated.

Linkage Geometry

Crank 279mm, rod 385mm, tab Z=702.9mm, motor Z=128.9mm — a constrained system. Change one value and every tilt angle changes. Calculated full forward kinematics.

Constrained Motion

The moving frame has exactly 2 DOF. The U-joint and two linkages together enforce this. Understanding how constraints reduce DOF is a core mechanical principle.

Pivots & U-joints

A U-joint transmits force across an angle. It has backlash, angular limits, and torque capacity — all verified for the 3/4-36 spline × 3/4 DD unit chosen.

Torque & Lever Arms

Pivot at X=200mm. CG ~X=800mm. ~180 lb total. Front pivot takes ~40%, leaving ~54 lb per rear motor. Lever calculation confirmed 250W motors are sufficient.

Crank Radius Tradeoffs

At 279mm, crank tip travels ±279mm in ±90° range. Larger = more tilt but more stress. Chosen to give ±8.8° without geometric over-centering at the extremes.

Why Geometry Can Fail

If the crank radius is too large, the linkage over-centers past vertical and locks or reverses. I verified the system stays within ±75° crank travel. Wrong = rig fails on first use.

Simple Physics

Simple ideas behind the motion.

This slide is not about scary formulas. It is just the everyday logic behind why the rig can feel real: brake feeling, turn feeling, a front hinge, and lever arms.

Braking / accelerating

When you brake, your body wants to move forward. When you accelerate, your body wants to move back. Pitch adds a small tilt to fake that feeling.

Turning

In a turn, your body expects some left/right lean. Roll adds that gentle lean so cornering feels more believable instead of totally flat.

Front U-joint = hinge

Think of the front U-joint like a door hinge. The rear motors do not need to lift the whole rig straight up. They mainly tip it around that front connection.

Levers save motor force

The crank arms and rods work like lever arms. Good geometry helps the motors create enough useful motion without needing huge industrial hardware.

Why this matters for buying parts

The goal is not maximum motion. The goal is enough motion to feel realistic, while still staying safe, controllable, and affordable. That is why the design focuses on the most useful cues instead of the most extreme movement.

Engineering Judgment

Trade-offs & logic.

Not a kit build. Each choice was a real engineering tradeoff — wrong decision = real cost.

Full rig vs seat mover?

Seat movers leave wheel and pedals stationary — mismatch makes motion feel wrong. A full rig moves everything coherently.

Chose: Full rig mover
Wiper motors vs linear actuators?

$20 actuators: ~5W effective, 25mm/s. 250W wiper + 279mm crank: 300mm/s, 50× more power. Mathematically eliminated.

Chose: Wiper motors (50× faster)
Front pivot vs two rear pivots?

With pivot at X=200mm, rear motors only see ~60% of total load. Two rear pivots = motors lift 100% of weight.

Chose: One front U-joint
Wood vs welded steel?

~$30 vs ~$150+. Rework: re-drill ($0) vs re-weld ($30+). Lighter moving frame. 2×4 handles the forces easily.

Chose: 2×4 lumber
Sabertooth vs Cytron MDDS30?

Cytron ($79) has no regen braking → back-EMF spikes PSU on deceleration. $46 premium on a 50A system is worth it.

Chose: Sabertooth 2×32
Fixed vs adjustable linkage?

Fixed rods: off by 5mm → re-drill the crank. Aramox adjustable: dial in neutral after assembly. ~$5 more. No reason not to.

Chose: Aramox 370–400mm
